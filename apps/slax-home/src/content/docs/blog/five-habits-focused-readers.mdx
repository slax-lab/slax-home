The average adult reads between 200 and 300 words per minute. At that pace, a well-written long-form article takes maybe 8 minutes to finish.

Yet most of us never get there. We open the article, read the first three paragraphs, check a notification, scroll back up to find our place, get distracted again, and eventually close the tab with the vague intention of "finishing it later."

Later never comes.

The problem isn't willpower. It's **environment and habit design**. Here are five habits that reliably improve both reading focus and retention.

## 1. Separate Collecting from Reading

The moment you open an article is almost never the right moment to read it. You're in discovery mode — following links, scanning headlines, riding a wave of curiosity. Switching into *deep reading* mode takes a gear change that most people never make.

> Save now, read later. When you're ready to read, read only.

Tools like Slax Reader exist precisely for this separation. When you see something worth reading, save it in two seconds with the browser extension. Later, when you sit down with clear intention and no other tabs open, that's when you actually read it.

The collection queue becomes a curated reading list — far better than relying on browser tabs or bookmarks.

## 2. Read with a Question in Mind

Passive reading is the enemy of retention. Before you start an article, write down — even just mentally — one question you're hoping the piece will answer.

This question acts as an **active filter**. Instead of processing every sentence equally, your brain is automatically scanning for relevant information. The signal-to-noise ratio of what you remember goes up dramatically.

If you can't articulate a question, consider whether the article deserves your time at all.

## 3. Highlight Sparingly, Annotate Liberally

Most people highlight too much. A page with 12 highlighted sentences hasn't been processed — it's just been scanned with a yellow pen. Highlights are only useful if you review them, and you won't review a wall of yellow text.

A better rule: **highlight a maximum of one passage per page** — the single most important sentence or idea. Then write one sentence of annotation *in your own words* explaining why it matters.

That one sentence of your own thinking is worth ten times the highlighted text.

Slax Reader makes this easy: select any passage, add an inline note, and it's saved in context alongside the original article.

## 4. Build a Deliberate Review Habit

Forgetting is not failure — it's the default behaviour of memory. The antidote isn't reading harder, it's **spaced review**.

Every week, spend 15 minutes looking at your recent highlights and notes. You don't need to re-read the full article. Just glance at your highlights and annotations. This single habit, practiced consistently, will triple what you actually retain.

Slax Note surfaces your recent saves and makes this review session fast and low-friction. The goal isn't to re-process everything — just to glance and reconnect.

## 5. Write One Sentence After Every Article

At the end of each piece you read, write one sentence:

> *"The main thing I want to remember from this is ________."*

That's it. One sentence. It forces your brain to synthesise and prioritise. It creates a retrievable summary that future-you will thank present-you for.

If you can't write that sentence, the article probably didn't land. Which is useful information too.

---

## The Underlying Principle

All five habits share a common logic: **make reading active, not passive**. Your brain doesn't retain information it processes passively. It retains information it interacts with — questions, annotations, summaries, connections.

The tools matter, but the habits matter more. Start with one. Try "write one sentence after every article" this week. It takes 30 seconds and will change how you read.
