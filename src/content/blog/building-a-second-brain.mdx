We consume enormous amounts of information every day — articles, newsletters, research papers, YouTube transcripts, forum threads. Most of it disappears within hours.

The problem isn't attention. It's architecture.

## Why Information Doesn't Stick

Traditional reading is passive. You scroll, skim, maybe highlight a sentence, then close the tab. There's no feedback loop that forces your brain to process the idea, connect it to something you already know, or decide where it belongs in your mental model.

What you need isn't more discipline. You need a **system that does the remembering for you** — what Tiago Forte famously calls a *second brain*.

## The Capture → Connect → Create Loop

A working second brain has three stages:

1. **Capture** — Pull interesting content out of the stream and into a dedicated space before it's gone.
2. **Connect** — Link that content to related ideas, questions, or projects you're already thinking about.
3. **Create** — Draw on your stored knowledge to write, decide, and build.

Slax is built around exactly this loop.

### Stage 1 — Capture with Slax Reader

[Slax Reader](https://slax.com/reader) is a read-later app with a difference: it's designed for *re-reading*, not just archiving.

When you save an article, Slax strips the clutter and renders a clean reading view. You can:

- **Highlight** any passage — the selection is saved with its surrounding context so it still makes sense weeks later.
- **Add inline notes** right next to the text, not in a separate sidebar that you'll never open.
- **Tag and organise** by topic before you've even finished reading.

The key is friction reduction. If saving something takes more than two seconds, you won't do it consistently. The Slax browser extension makes capture nearly instant.

### Stage 2 — Connect with Slax Note

Captured content only becomes useful when you start seeing patterns across pieces. That's where [Slax Note](https://slax.com/note) comes in.

When you move a highlight or article into Slax Note, you're not just filing it — you're inviting connections. The editor nudges you to:

- Write a **short summary in your own words** (the best test of whether you understood something)
- Ask **what this reminds you of** — linking new ideas to existing notes
- Tag with **project context** so the note surfaces when you're actively working on something relevant

Over time, your note graph becomes a genuine thinking tool, not a read-later graveyard.

### Stage 3 — Create

The third stage is where the system pays off. When you sit down to write a blog post, prepare a presentation, or make a decision, you're not starting from a blank page. You're drawing on months of curated thinking.

Search your notes. Follow the links. Let your past self brief your present self.

## A Practical Workflow

Here's the daily loop I use:

```
Morning reading session (20 min)
  → Save 3–5 interesting articles to Slax Reader
  → Highlight one key passage per article

Afternoon processing (10 min)
  → Open Slax Note
  → For each highlight, write 2–3 sentences in my own words
  → Link to any related notes

Weekly review (30 min)
  → Look at everything tagged for active projects
  → Identify emerging themes
  → Write one short synthesis note
```

The weekly synthesis note is the highest-leverage activity in the loop. It forces you to zoom out from individual pieces and notice what your thinking is actually converging on.

## The Compounding Effect

A second brain gets more valuable over time because of compounding connections. A note you wrote six months ago about cognitive load theory suddenly becomes highly relevant when you're designing an onboarding flow. That connection only exists because you captured and connected the idea when it was fresh.

Start small. Capture one article today. Write two sentences about it. Link it to something you already know.

The architecture does the rest.
