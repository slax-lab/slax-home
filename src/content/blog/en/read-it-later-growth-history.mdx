Across [twenty years of read-it-later products](/blog/read-it-later-history/), the first users came from the same four places, over and over: the founder's own reputation, tech press coverage, app store featuring, and buttons embedded in other people's apps. Paid advertising doesn't make the list.

## Three ways the first generation started

**Instapaper: the founder was the distribution.** When Marco Arment released Instapaper in January 2008, he was Tumblr's CTO, and his blog marco.org had a steady readership in the tech world. Within two days of launch, Daring Fireball and TechCrunch had both covered it. When the App Store opened in July 2008, Instapaper was in the first batch and caught the opening-day wave: over 2,000 downloads on day one, over 15,000 in the first month, and repeat featuring from Apple after that.

**Read It Later, later Pocket: put the button inside other apps.** Nate Weiner started with a Firefox extension in 2007 and found his first users through coverage on productivity sites like Lifehacker. The real lever came around 2010, when he wrote and open-sourced a toolkit called ShareKit: iOS developers added one line of code and got a complete share menu for their app — and Read It Later sat in that menu's default list, right beside Twitter and Facebook. Hundreds of apps adopted ShareKit, and every one of them handed Read It Later new users for free.

**Readability: spread on its visible effect.** Arc90's 2009 bookmarklet did one thing — wash a cluttered page into a column of clean text. The effect was immediate, so users passed it around on their own. Apple later used its open-source algorithm in Safari's Reader mode. Its strongest technology was something anyone could take.

## Paid downloads blocked growth

On how to charge and when to charge, the first generation paid its tuition.

Instapaper charged first: a $9.99 Pro app, cut to $4.99 in 2009, plus an optional $1/month subscription in 2010. Brian Donohue, who later ran the product, was blunt in retrospect: once free competitors like Pocket and Readability existed, the paid download became a visible drag on growth. In late 2013, Instapaper took an App Store "Free App of the Week" slot — that one week brought more downloads than the previous two years combined, and subscriptions rose 10–15%.

Pocket went the opposite direction in April 2012: rename, rebuild, fully free, with roughly $14.5 million in venture funding covering the costs. It had about 4.5 million users at the rename and doubled within months. By the end of 2012, more than ten thousand developers had hooked into its open API; in 2015, Firefox built it into the browser.

Free bought scale. It didn't buy income — Pocket ended up acquired by Mozilla, then shut down. Charge upfront and you block growth; stay free and you starve. The first generation never solved that one. The products that survived later mostly landed on the same answer: let people bring their content in free, and charge a subscription for the deeper features. [We've done that math separately](/blog/how-to-price-a-read-later-app-that-outlives-you/).

## After the platforms gave the basics away

In 2011, Apple built Reading List into Safari on iOS 5. The feature itself was plain, but it shipped with the OS, needed no account, and sat in everyone's browser. From that day on, "save + sync + offline" alone earned nothing; an independent tool had to go visibly deeper.

Evernote's Web Clipper showed a different shape entirely. Clipping was never meant to be a business — it was the intake pipe for Evernote's freemium machine. The more you clipped, the heavier your notebook, the harder it was to leave, the likelier you'd pay. Evernote went from 1 million registered users in May 2009 to 10 million in June 2011, with the clipper as its highest-frequency entrance. Read-later as a feature inside a bigger product: that route worked too.

## The new generation's three routes

**Readwise Reader: move users in from the next room.** Readwise started in 2017 with highlight syncing and review, and built a base of heavy readers already paying monthly. When Reader entered public beta in late 2022, its first users came straight from that base — thousands of long-term testers, no traffic purchased. Add manifesto-style launch posts and a relentless changelog, and the company built all of it without venture money.

**Matter: start from picked content.** Two founders out of Nextdoor, Y Combinator Summer 2020, a $2 million seed and a $7 million Series A led by GV. Its cold start was hand-picked content: recommendations drawn from a set of credible Twitter writers and researchers, so the app held something worth reading the moment you opened it — then came saving and reading.

**Omnivore: the open-source community route.** A GitHub repo, Hacker News, a team newsletter, an iOS beta — drawing exactly the users who care about open source, self-hosting, and knowledge workflows. The user growth was real; the revenue was donations on Open Collective. In October 2024, ElevenLabs hired the team away, and the cloud service closed within a month. Community brought users. It didn't bring salaries.

## What still works, and what doesn't run anymore

Some plays were bound to their era:

- The App Store's opening-batch featuring — 2008 happened once;
- Launching off one or two authoritative blogs — Instapaper got covered by Daring Fireball and TechCrunch within two days of launch; that treatment rarely lands for a new product now;
- The "mobile web is unbearable" pain — 4G, 5G, and built-in reader modes flattened it;
- A browser handing you its front door, the way Firefox did for Pocket.

And a list that keeps recurring and still works:

- **Make saving a reflex.** Instapaper's bookmarklet, Pocket's share button, Evernote's clipper — each one starts by cutting the friction of saving. The metric is plain: can a new user complete three saves on day one?
- **Take more sources.** Beyond web pages, accept at least two of: newsletters, PDFs, video transcripts. A product that only saves URLs is competing with the browser's bookmark bar.
- **Let highlights flow out.** One click into Notion or Obsidian turns the tool from one-off consumption into a long-term asset — which is what holds up a subscription.
- **Curate before you socialize.** Matter's lesson: hand-pick a small set of high-quality sources, verify that recommended pieces actually get opened, then think about open community features.
- **Build in the open, for heavy users.** Roadmaps, changelogs, long-running answers on HN and Reddit, a small deep beta. That's where Instapaper's, Reader's, and Omnivore's first users came from.

Slax Reader is learning from this list and putting it into practice. We plan to do this for a long time.
