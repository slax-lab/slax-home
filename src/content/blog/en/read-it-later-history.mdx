On November 12, 2025, Pocket closed its last data-export window. Anyone who missed it lost their reading list for good — years of saved articles, permanently deleted. The product had launched in 2007, grown past 20 million registered users, and been acquired by Mozilla in 2017. It was the biggest name "read it later" ever had. From shutdown announcement to data deletion took less than six months.

Pocket is gone. The problem it solved is still here: you run into an article you want, you have no time now, so you save it and read it later in a clean, quiet interface. One click to save, automatic article extraction, no ads, synced across your devices, readable offline — that's what a read-it-later app does.

People have been building this product for twenty years. The builders changed, batch after batch. Few are still standing.

## Wave 1 (2003–2012): from bookmarks to dedicated tools

The story starts with bookmarks. In 2003, del.icio.us moved bookmarks from the browser into the cloud and added tags. Founder Joshua Schachter had a plain explanation for why people save a page: either you think you'll need it again, or you have no time to read it now and want to read it later. The community soon settled on a "toread" tag for exactly that.

In August 2007, Nate Weiner, an independent developer in Minnesota, got tired of emailing himself links and wrote a Firefox extension with a plain name: Read It Later. In January 2008, Tumblr co-founder Marco Arment launched Instapaper — he rode the New York subway every day, underground with no signal, and needed to carry the day's long reads with him. In 2009, the design firm Arc90 released Readability, which did one thing: strip an ad-cluttered page down to a single column of clean text.

Who was first? The two sides argued for years. Weiner's product shipped earlier, but began as little more than a local bookmark folder. Arment held that a real read-it-later service means multi-device sync, article extraction, and offline caching — and that Instapaper assembled those pieces first.

Readability open-sourced its article-extraction algorithm in 2009. Apple used it for Safari's Reader mode in 2010, and Mozilla's fork still powers Firefox's Reader View today. "Clean reading" turned into a built-in browser feature, step by step — one less reason to pay an independent tool.

## Wave 2 (2011–2017): platforms move in, acquisitions begin

In 2011, Apple added Reading List to Safari in iOS 5. Nothing to install, no account to create: save a page, it syncs across devices, it reads offline. It did less than the dedicated tools, but it was free, preinstalled, and took zero learning.

In April 2012, Read It Later renamed itself Pocket and went fully free, trading revenue for scale on roughly $14.5 million of venture funding. Registered users grew from about 4.5 million at the rename to over 20 million by 2017. In 2015, Firefox built Pocket into the browser. In February 2017, Mozilla bought the whole company — the first acquisition in Mozilla's history.

Instapaper went another way: Arment sold a majority stake to the incubator Betaworks in 2013, and Pinterest acquired it in 2016. Readability never found a buyer. It had tried an idealistic revenue model — 70% of subscription fees would go to writers — and pleased no one: publishers resented a middleman charging on top of their content, and Apple's in-app purchase rules took another 30%. In September 2016, Readability shut down.

By 2017, all three pioneers had been sold or were gone: Pocket belonged to Mozilla, Instapaper to Pinterest, and Readability had closed.

## Wave 3 (the 2020s): a revival, then the shutdowns

The 2020s brought a new generation that extended saving beyond web pages — to newsletters, PDFs, podcasts, and video. Matter (public beta in 2021, a $7 million Series A led by GV) pulled newsletters, podcasts, and Twitter threads into one queue, with text-to-speech close to a human voice. Readwise Reader (public beta in 2022) chained highlights, notes, and review into one flow, so what you read lands in your knowledge base; the company took no venture money and lives on subscriptions. Omnivore (2021) open-sourced the whole stack and gave it away free.

Then the shutdowns came one after another. In October 2024, the AI voice company ElevenLabs acqui-hired the Omnivore team — it bought the people. The product closed within a month, and users got a deadline to export. In May 2025, Mozilla announced the end of Pocket; the official blog said "the way people save and consume content on the web has evolved." Service stopped on July 8. The same month, e-reader maker Kobo announced what would replace Pocket on its devices: Instapaper. The veteran of 2008 turned out to be the answer of 2025.

China ran the same story with one extra force. Cubox launched in 2020 as a read-it-later app and now sells itself as AI reading; the indie developer behind SimpRead hand-tuned article parsing for hundreds of Chinese sites, one site at a time. But the tool that actually catches most people's "read this later" urge is WeChat's floating window: tap once and the article shrinks to a bubble you can reopen anytime. A super-app's built-in shortcut squeezed the dedicated tools — the same squeeze Safari's Reading List had applied a decade earlier.

## Why this business keeps dying

Products died batch after batch. The deaths look different; the causes repeat.

**Saved, never read.** Saving is effortless and reading takes work, so queues grow until users wipe the whole list in guilt and uninstall. Low open rates mean low retention, and low retention means subscriptions don't get paid. This is the root of the category.

**Free doesn't hold.** Pocket traded free for scale, and scale never turned into revenue; Mozilla carried it, and when Mozilla cut back in 2025, it had no way to survive on its own. Omnivore was open source and free; nobody paid the server bills, and when the team got bought, users saw the other side of "free": it can stop at any time.

**Platforms give the basics away.** Safari and Chrome ship reading lists. WeChat ships a floating window. Note apps ship web clippers. The extraction algorithm is open source. Once the basics are free everywhere, an independent tool has to be clearly better before it can charge anything at all.

**Acquisition usually means shutdown.** del.icio.us changed hands five times and died. Omnivore closed a month after being bought. Pocket lived eight years under Mozilla and was still shut down. Big companies buy small tools for the technology or the team, and the product fades afterward. Twenty years produced exactly one counterexample: Instapaper — sold to Pinterest, bought back by its operating team in 2018, independent ever since.

## What the survivors look like

The ones still alive in 2026 run the same way.

Instapaper: a small team, subscription-funded, eighteen years without a gap. Pinboard: a one-person bookmarking service where signing up costs money; founder Maciej Cegłowski makes a steady $200,000+ a year from it. wallabag: an open-source project a French developer started in 2013, installable on your own server, with an €11/year hosted version. Readwise Reader: subscription-funded, not cheap, and built reading into the front door of knowledge management. Raindrop.io: a one-person bookmark manager, never funded, with all revenue from subscriptions.

Their common traits: users pay directly, and the money covers the team; data exports anytime; the team stays small and cheap to run, so it doesn't need explosive growth to stay alive.

## Four things to check before you switch

The survivor profile flips into a checklist. Before you move into a read-later app, look at four things:

1. **Can you export your data, anytime?** In formats other tools read (HTML, CSV, Markdown). Pocket users got nearly six months of export window; Omnivore users got one.
2. **Is it open source? Can you self-host?** If the service ends, does the code survive — and can you keep it running yourself?
3. **How does it make money?** If it's free, work out who is paying for it. If it's subscriptions, check whether the price can plausibly support the team.
4. **Does it work offline?** Open it in airplane mode, make a highlight, close and reopen. [We ran this test on 14 apps](/blog/local-first-slax-reader/).

## FAQ

**What is a read-it-later app?**
A tool that saves pages you have no time for now, so you can read them later in a clean interface. The standard kit: article extraction, cross-device sync, offline reading, highlights.

**Is Pocket really shut down?**
Yes. Service ended on July 8, 2025, and all user data was deleted on November 12, 2025. Kobo picked Instapaper to replace it on its e-readers.

**Are there free read-later apps?**
Several kinds: the reading lists built into Safari and Chrome; open-source, self-hostable wallabag and Slax Reader; and the free tiers of the commercial apps. The differences are feature ceilings and who controls the data.

**What replaced Pocket?**
Instapaper, Readwise Reader, Raindrop.io, wallabag, and Slax Reader all cover the basics, with different emphases. [Full comparisons here](/reader/alternatives/).
