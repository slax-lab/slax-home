## What Raindrop Does Well

Let's be honest about this.

Raindrop is probably the best-looking bookmark library you can own. Custom covers, grid and gallery views, nested collections, tags, filters — if you enjoy a beautifully organized archive, nothing else in this category comes close. The Pro tier adds the housekeeping tools serious collectors actually use: duplicate detection, broken-link cleanup, automatic backups, permanent copies.

It's also a model indie product: one developer for over a decade, sustained entirely by subscriptions. At $27.99 a year, Pro is one of the cheapest paid tiers in the category.

**If a well-organized library is the goal, Raindrop is the best at it.**

## Organizing and Reading Are Different Jobs

Here's the catch: organizing links and reading them are different jobs.

Raindrop's reading experience is functional — there's a reader view, and Pro users get highlights. But reading is the side feature. There's no AI to help you triage fifty saved articles. No outline to navigate a 5,000-word essay. And the whole app assumes you're online.

That last part comes straight from Raindrop's own FAQ:

> "An internet connection is required to sync changes and save new bookmarks."

We ran the test ourselves for [our local-first comparison of 14 read-later apps](/blog/local-first-slax-reader/): open Raindrop, turn off Wi-Fi, select text, hit highlight. The highlight appears — then you close the app, reopen it, and it's gone. It never saved.

Slax Reader is built local-first. Same test: the highlight shows up, saves on the device, and syncs when you're back online. Subway, flight, dead zone — reading doesn't stop.

We'll concede this one upfront: Raindrop wins on organization. Nested collections, visual boards, filters, batch editing — it's a filing system with real depth.

If your workflow is "file everything in its right place," Raindrop fits. If it's "save now, find it when I need it," Slax's free search covers it.
